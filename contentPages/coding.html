Software Engineering Experience

I have been writing code in one fashion or another for well over a decade, but my true professional engineering experience began in 2013. That year, a consumer cloud storage company called MiMedia took a gamble on a fresh Master's grad, and gave me the greatest opportunity to become a software professional that anyone could hope for. I started out just hoping for a job that could make me a decent living, and ended with a love of programming and building new things. This has carried me through my stint in academia, and I hope to continue loving what I do for the rest of my engineering career. Here, I'm going to tell the story of how I built up the skills I have. You can check my LinkedIn for a more thorough list of my professional accomplishments.

Before my big break (2010 - 2012)

Sometime in undergrad — so before 2010 — I began using Linux as my main OS for both personal and work purposes. I don't remember any big reason for making the switch. Very likely I just thought it would be a fun thing to try, and decided to keep doing it after a while. It was also in undergrad that I made my first few steps into using Python, though it was really during my Master's degree, starting in 2010, when I began using it extensively. Our physics graduate student orientation at Drexel (where I did my Master's) included a brief introduction to Python, and I wound up using it for calculations in both homework assignments and research. I also started using Git at this time.

The big break (2013 - 2016)

I joined MiMedia during a time when they were beginning to revamp their entire product, including their web app, mobile apps, and platform. I was briefly an intern, but I learned fast enough that I soon became a full-time backend engineer. We primarily worked in Java, though I continued using Python when I needed a quick proof-of-concept or to sketch out some functionality. Our development cycle followed an Agile pattern, and I learned to be meticulous about unit testing. I also got very comfortable with SQL, and constantly used Spring Framework.

One of my largest projects was building the server-side platform for video-on-demand (VOD) streaming — since we were a consumer cloud storage solution, we needed our users to be able to stream their own videos to any of their devices through our app. I was lucky enough to be part of this project from the very beginning, from deciding which streaming protocols to support to implementing them, and even developing a process for ingesting user video content so it could be streamed efficiently. I became fluent in the HLS protocol and the MP4 file format. In learning about these things and how a video file might be prepared for streaming, I made regular use of FFmpeg.

The academic detour (2016 - 2023)

Although I was enjoying my work in industry, I had always felt like I had unfinished business in physics, and in 2016 I decided it was time to get a PhD. The majority of my doctoral research was part of the Mu2e Experiment, a high-energy physics experiment at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory, whose goal is to make a precision measurement of an extremely rare, never-before-seen conversion of a muon to an electron. The collaboration used C++ for their simulation and event-reconstruction code, using the Geant4 toolkit for the physics simulation portion. However, Python remained my language of choice, and I used it to perform calculations and analysis as often as possible. Luckily for me, there are Python bindings for ROOT, our data analysis framework. I used LaTeX markup to prepare clean-looking reports, including my dissertation. Even now, I use it to format my resume.

After contributing a model of my own thesis work's detector system to the main codebase, I was asked to take on the role of Continuous Integration manager. The collaboration had been looking for someone to replace the previous CI manager, who was graduating, and thought I fit the bill. This allowed me to get much more familiar with the Jenkins workflows used to perform tests on code contributions, and the way we used the GitHub API to get information about pull requests and communicate with PR authors. The code that controlled this communication, and the tests themselves, was written in a combination of python and Bash. I had used Bash before to write the odd install or start script, but this got me much more comfortable with it.

Where I am now (2023 - present)

Eventually, grad students have to graduate, and I was no exception. I defended my dissertation on December 16, 2022, and officially received my PhD in February of 2023. I am currently a software engineer at Courier Health, a healthcare startup focusing on patient services in biopharma. Despite no longer officially being a student, I have not stopped learning. I'm currently building my knowlege of modern cloud infrastructure in two ways. The first is professional experience; Courier Health makes extensive use of Amazon's AWS infrastructure, including taking an infrastructure as code approach to ensuring the environment is set up correctly and reproducibly. I have also been working on my own. This site is currently hosted on Azure, and includes both a database and object storage backend, all hosted in the cloud (and written using Django).
